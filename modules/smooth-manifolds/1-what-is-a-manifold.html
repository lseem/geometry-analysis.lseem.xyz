1. What is a manifold?

A manifold is the main object of study of geometers and topologists. Manifolds have a notion of dimension, which is constant across the whole manifold. Informally, we can think of dimension as the number of “local degrees of freedom” (this particular point of view will be formalised in the notion of a tangent space). Another way of viewing this is that manifolds “locally look like” patches of $\R^n$. We often call a manifold of dimension $n$ an $n$-manifold.

For example, $\R^n$ is a manifold of dimension $n$, a torus $T^2$ is a manifold of dimension $2$, a sphere $S^2$ is a manifold of dimension $2$, etc. What other manifolds can you think of?

In a course on surfaces in $\R^3$, all the surfaces that you have seen are examples of manifolds. The difference in this course is that we will be thinking of manifolds as intrinsic objects, rather than as objects that sit inside some ambient space. There are many reasons for doing this, a couple of which are:

  1. Viewing manifolds intrinsically rather than as a subset of some ambient space helps us figure out which features of a manifold are intrinsic to the manifold or are just an effect of how we have put this manifold into the ambient space.
  2. In general relativity, we model the universe as a manifold. Since it is impossible for us to leave the universe and view it from “outside” as sitting in some larger ambient space, we have to develop mathematical tools for dealing with the geometry and topology of manifolds from an intrinsic point of view.
  3. It doesn’t cost us much to deal with manifolds intrinsically.
  4. A priori, we can’t be sure that every manifold can be easily sat inside an ambient space – this is a theorem that we have to prove.

Many other examples are provided by physics; for example, the phase space of classical systems form a manifold. We will also see that if a group acts sufficiently nicely on the manifold, then the quotient space is a manifold (think of a torus as $\R^2/\Z^2$, or the circle as $\R/\Z$).

In this mini-course, we won’t be dealing in the generality of a topological manifold; rather, we will be focusing on smooth manifolds. Smooth manifolds are topological manifolds with an extra structure, that allows us to differentiate functions on the manifold. For example, modelling the Earth as a sphere, we could take $t:S^2\to\R$ to be the surface temperature at each point (in Kelvin of course!). Certainly we would like to be able to differentiate such a function.

After this mini-course you could go in the direction of differential/algebraic topology. Stokes’s theorem, which is a general version of the fundamental theorem of calculus and unifies many of the theorems from multivariable calculus (e.g. Green’s theorem, the divergence theorem, etc) gives a starting point for building a very powerful topological tool called the de Rham cohomology.

Alternatively, you could study some introductory Riemannian geometry, where we develop the notions of lengths of curves, area, and (importantly) curvature, culminating in the famous Gauss-Bonnet theorem; this content is a generalisation of, and more similar in spirit to, the material found in a course on surfaces in $\R^3$.
